Core Concept

Why Money Costs Money

Return is the reward for taking on Risk. The more uncertain an investor feels about getting their money back, the higher the return they will demand.

The Rs. 50 Million Question

Two Very Different Promises

Option A: Debt (Bank Loan) @ 12%

  • Fixed interest must be paid, regardless of profit.
  • Bank owns no part of the business.
  • Lender is repaid first if things go wrong.
  • Usually secured by assets (collateral).
  • Lower risk to the lender = Lower Cost (12%).

Option B: Equity (Shares) @ 18%

  • No fixed repayment; returns rely on profit.
  • Investors own a slice of the business.
  • Shareholders are repaid last in bankruptcy.
  • No collateral backup.
  • High risk to the investor = Higher Cost (18%).

Answering Question 2

Why the 6% Gap?

1. WHO GETS PAID FIRST? If the company fails, the bank legally gets their money back first. Shareholders are last in line. Because shareholders take more risk, they demand a higher reward (18%).
2. GUARANTEED PAYMENTS The company must pay the bank interest every month no matter what. But paying shareholders is optional. Uncertainty demands a premium.
3. BACKUP SECURITY Bank loans are backed by company assets (like buildings). If the company can't pay, the bank takes the building. Equity has no backup safety net.

Answering Question 1

Our Recommendation

Cost Comparison

  • If the company is mature (like PickMe): We recommend Option A (12% Debt). It is significantly cheaper, and steady cash flows reduce the risk of defaulting.
  • If the company is a startup (like Lincro): We recommend Option B (18% Equity). Although it is more expensive on paper, equity is flexible. It avoids the deadly risk of bankruptcy if the startup fails to generate immediate cash for interest payments.
