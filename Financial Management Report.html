Cost of Capital, Risk, and Return:
A Comparative Analysis

Group 1 Submission

Question 1: Recommendation for Raising Rs. 50 Million

Scenario: A company wants to raise Rs. 50 million to expand its business. The finance manager is considering raising money through a bank loan (debt) at 12% interest, or issuing new shares (equity) to investors who expect an 18% return.

Our Recommendation:

As a group, our recommendation on whether the company should choose Option A (Debt at 12%) or Option B (Equity at 18%) depends entirely on the stage, stability, and risk profile of the company. The decision cannot be made based on the cost of capital alone, because cost is directly tied to the underlying risk.

  • If the company is mature and established (e.g., PickMe): We strongly recommend Option A (Bank Loan at 12%). An established company generally has highly predictable, steady cash flows and substantial physical or digital assets. Because their risk of defaulting on loan payments is low, they can safely take advantage of the cheaper 12% cost of capital. The fixed interest payments will not heavily strain their operations, allowing them to maximize value for their existing shareholders.
  • If the company is an early-stage startup (e.g., Lincro.lk): We strongly recommend Option B (Equity at 18%). Startups inherently possess unpredictable cash flows and often lack physical collateral. Taking on a bank loan forces the company to make rigid, mandatory monthly interest payments. If the expansion project takes longer than expected to generate revenue, the startup risks defaulting and facing bankruptcy. By issuing equity, the company gains flexible capital; shareholders only receive dividends when the company actually turns a profit. Although 18% is mathematically more expensive, it provides the vital safety net a startup requires to survive its growth phase.

Question 2: Why Cost of Capital Differs Between Debt and Equity

Scenario: The finance manager notes that debt requires paying fixed interest, while equity requires giving returns based on profits. Each option carries different risks and costs.

The core principle of financial management dictates that return is the reward for taking on risk. The 6% gap between the cost of debt (12%) and the cost of equity (18%) exists solely because an equity investor is exposed to a significantly higher level of risk than a bank.

Risk influences the expected return in three primary ways:

  • 1. Who Gets Paid First? If the company fails, the bank legally gets their money back first. Shareholders are last in line. Because shareholders take more risk by being last, they demand a higher reward (18%).
  • 2. Guaranteed Payments: The company must pay the bank interest every month no matter what. But paying dividends to shareholders is optional and depends on profit. Investors demand a higher premium for accepting this uncertainty.
  • 3. Backup Security: Bank loans are backed by company assets (like buildings). If the company can't pay, the bank takes the building. Equity investments have no backup safety net, so they expect a higher return.

Ultimately, from the perspective of the provider of funds, equity is riskier than debt. Therefore, to attract equity investors, a company must offer a higher potential return than what they would offer a bank.
